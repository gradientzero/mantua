"Small" is a moving target. Models that were frontier-scale three years ago now run on a
single GPU. For this research project, a **small language model** is defined less by
parameter count and more by three practical properties:

1. **Deployable inside an enterprise perimeter** — on-prem or in a private VPC, on
   hardware a mid-size company can realistically procure. See [[private-deployment]].
2. **Cost profile compatible with high-volume, low-margin tasks** — per-token economics
   that make continuous background processing viable, not just interactive chat.
3. **Specialized, not general** — tuned for a narrow set of industry-specific tasks,
   usually via [[model-distillation|distillation]] or fine-tuning, and evaluated only
   against those tasks.

Under this definition the interesting range today is roughly 0.5B–15B parameters, but the
number is a consequence of the three properties, not the definition itself.

## Why the distinction matters

The strategic question for enterprises is not "big model vs small model" but *which tasks
justify frontier-model dependence and which don't*. A claims-processing pipeline that
runs a million documents a month has a very different calculus from a monthly board-memo
drafting task.
